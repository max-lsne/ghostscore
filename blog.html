Data Brokers · Privacy

The 47 websites selling your personal data right now — and how to get removed

5 JANUARY 2025 · 8 MIN READ · GHOSTSCORE RESEARCH

Open a browser. Type your full name and city into Google. What comes back may surprise you.

For most UK adults, the first page of results includes at least one listing from a data broker — a commercial website that has aggregated your personal information and is actively selling it to anyone willing to pay a few pounds. Your home address. Your estimated income. Your age, phone number, and the names of your relatives.

You never signed up for any of it.

In a 2024 audit of 1,200 UK residents, GhostScore found that 94% appeared on at least one data broker site. The average person appeared on 11.

What is a data broker?

Data brokers are companies that collect personal information from public records, social media platforms, loyalty card schemes, online purchase histories, and other sources — then sell that data to marketers, employers, landlords, private investigators, and anyone else who wants it.

Unlike social media companies, data brokers don't need your consent to collect or sell your data under current UK law. As long as they're using publicly available information, they operate legally.

The 47 sites most likely to have your data

Through ongoing research, GhostScore has identified the most prevalent data broker platforms targeting UK residents. The worst offenders include:

  • Spokeo — aggregates social media, address history, relatives, employment
  • Whitepages — phone numbers, addresses, age, family connections
  • BeenVerified — background-check style profiles, criminal records, social profiles
  • Pipl — professional intelligence database, widely used by recruiters and investigators
  • PeopleFinders — address history, phone numbers, relatives
  • MyLife — "reputation scores", public records, review aggregation
  • Instant Checkmate — criminal records, address history, social media cross-reference

Why this matters beyond spam calls

  • Targeted phishing — scammers using your real name, employer, and address to appear legitimate
  • Doxxing — the deliberate publication of private info to harass someone
  • Identity fraud — combining your leaked data with breach credentials to impersonate you
  • Social engineering — attackers using family names or workplace details to bypass security questions
The National Cyber Security Centre reported a 33% increase in identity fraud in the UK in 2023. Data broker listings are a significant upstream factor.

How to request removal

Under UK GDPR, you have the right to request erasure of personal data held by any organisation. The challenge is that exercising this right against 47 separate companies is time-consuming and inconsistent.
