Data Brokers · Privacy

The 47 websites selling your personal data right now — and how to get removed

5 JANUARY 2025 · 8 MIN READ · GHOSTSCORE RESEARCH

Open a browser. Type your full name and city into Google. What comes back may surprise you.

For most UK adults, the first page of results includes at least one listing from a data broker — a commercial website that has aggregated your personal information and is actively selling it to anyone willing to pay a few pounds. Your home address. Your estimated income. Your age, phone number, and the names of your relatives.

You never signed up for any of it.

In a 2024 audit of 1,200 UK residents, GhostScore found that 94% appeared on at least one data broker site. The average person appeared on 11.

What is a data broker?

Data brokers are companies that collect personal information from public records, social media platforms, loyalty card schemes, online purchase histories, and other sources — then sell that data to marketers, employers, landlords, private investigators, and anyone else who wants it.

Unlike social media companies, data brokers don't need your consent to collect or sell your data under current UK law. As long as they're using publicly available information, they operate legally. This is why your electoral roll registration, Companies House directorship, or old social media profile can all become data broker content without your knowledge.

The 47 sites most likely to have your data

Through ongoing research, GhostScore has identified the most prevalent data broker platforms targeting UK residents. The worst offenders — those with the largest UK databases and the most detailed profiles — include:

  • Spokeo — aggregates social media, address history, relatives, employment
  • Whitepages — phone numbers, addresses, age, family connections
  • BeenVerified — background-check style profiles, criminal records, social profiles
  • Pipl — professional intelligence database, widely used by recruiters and investigators
  • PeopleFinders — address history, phone numbers, relatives
  • MyLife — "reputation scores", public records, review aggregation
  • Instant Checkmate — criminal records, address history, social media cross-reference

Beyond these seven, there are dozens of smaller regional brokers, UK-specific electoral roll aggregators, and niche platforms targeting professional data.

Why this matters beyond spam calls

Most people dismiss data broker listings as the source of annoying cold calls. The reality is more serious. Exposed personal data enables:

  • Targeted phishing — scammers using your real name, employer, and address to appear legitimate
  • Doxxing — the deliberate publication of private info to harass someone
  • Identity fraud — combining your leaked data with breach credentials to impersonate you
  • Social engineering — attackers using family names, previous addresses, or workplace details to bypass security questions
The National Cyber Security Centre reported a 33% increase in identity fraud in the UK in 2023. Data broker listings are a significant upstream factor.

How to request removal (and why it's harder than it should be)

Under UK GDPR, you have the right to request erasure of personal data held by any organisation. This is commonly called "the right to be forgotten." The challenge is that exercising this right against 47 separate companies is time-consuming, inconsistent, and requires follow-up. Each broker has their own opt-out process — some require ID verification, some ignore first requests, and some re-list you from new data sources after removal.

The effective approach is systematic: identify every source, submit removal requests with reference to UK GDPR Article 17, and follow up with the ICO if requests are ignored. GhostScore's PDF report includes pre-written GDPR removal templates for every broker where your data appears.

Inside a GhostScore audit: what we scan across 200+ sources

12 JANUARY 2025 · 5 MIN READ · GHOSTSCORE METHODOLOGY

When someone asks what GhostScore actually does, the short answer is: we systematically search for you across the entire public internet and score what we find. But the longer answer — the methodology behind a complete digital exposure audit — is worth understanding if you're serious about your privacy.

Here's exactly how a GhostScore report is produced.

The 8 scan modules

Module 1: Search Engine Presence

We run structured queries across Google, Bing, and DuckDuckGo using your name, location, employer, and key identifiers. We identify every page where you appear in the top 50 results, assess the sensitivity of each listing, and note whether any results enable aggregation — the ability to build a complete profile of you from multiple sources.

Module 2: Social Media Surface Area

Even private profiles leave public footprints. We scan LinkedIn, X/Twitter, Facebook, Instagram, TikTok, Reddit, and GitHub using site-specific queries to identify what's publicly indexed. Public posts, comment history, profile photos, and "mutual connections" data can all reveal more than intended.

Module 3: Data Broker Listings

We check your details across the 47 most significant UK-accessible data broker platforms. For each listing found, we log the specific data fields exposed and include a tailored removal request template in your report.

Module 4: AI Model Knowledge

This is the module most people don't think about. We query multiple large language models — including ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini — directly with your name. For public figures and professionals with significant online presence, AI models can recite biographical details, professional history, and sometimes personal information with surprising accuracy. This module assesses what AI "knows" about you and where that knowledge originates.

Module 5: Breach Database Exposure

Using the HaveIBeenPwned database and supplementary breach sources, we check whether your email addresses and phone numbers appear in known data breaches. We identify which specific breaches affected you and what data fields were compromised — passwords, dates of birth, physical addresses, payment card data.

Module 6: Public Records

UK public records are an often-overlooked source of personal data. We check Companies House for director listings (which include home addresses in some historical filings), the Electoral Roll, court records, and planning application databases.

Module 7: Reverse Image Search

We run your profile photos through Google Vision and TinEye to identify where your images appear online — including on sites you're unaware of or haven't authorised.

Module 8: Document & Metadata Analysis

Publicly accessible documents you've published — CVs, research papers, presentations — can contain EXIF metadata or embedded personal details that reveal location data, device information, and creation timestamps.

The GhostScore: 0–100

Each module contributes a weighted score to your overall GhostScore. The scale runs from 0 (Ghost — virtually no public footprint) to 100 (Fully Public — comprehensively indexed across all channels). Most people score between 35 and 65.

A high score isn't a verdict — it's a map. The report tells you exactly what's exposed, where it lives, and in what order to address it.

Case Study · Real Story

"I had no idea I was this exposed" — a GhostScore audit in real life

19 JANUARY 2025 · 6 MIN READ · CLIENT CASE STUDY

Sarah — not her real name — is a 38-year-old marketing director at a mid-size agency in London. She considers herself reasonably privacy-conscious: she doesn't post her home address online, her Instagram is private, and she uses a password manager. She thought her digital footprint was under control.

Her GhostScore came back at 74.

What we found

The audit identified 34 separate sources where Sarah's personal information was publicly accessible. The most significant:

  • Spokeo listed her current address, previous three addresses, estimated household income (£85,000–£100,000), the names of her husband and two children, and her employer
  • BeenVerified held a profile with her date of birth, phone number, and a record of a county court judgement from 2011 that she'd believed was spent
  • LinkedIn — her public profile — was being indexed with enough detail that querying her name and employer in ChatGPT returned an accurate summary of her career history and current role
  • Companies House showed a dormant limited company she'd set up in 2019, with her home address listed as the registered address — still publicly visible
  • An old forum post from 2009 on a now-defunct photography forum appeared in Google results, with her full name, hometown, and a photo she didn't recognise from that period of her life
"The Companies House thing was the worst. I genuinely forgot I'd used my home address. It had just been sitting there for five years."

The removal process

Sarah used the GDPR removal templates in her GhostScore report to contact each data broker directly. The process took roughly 3 hours across two evenings. Here's what happened:

  • Spokeo — removed within 72 hours following a GDPR Article 17 request
  • BeenVerified — removed within 5 business days, though she had to submit proof of identity
  • Companies House — she applied to suppress her home address using the Companies House suppression form; approved in 11 days
  • LinkedIn — she adjusted her privacy settings to limit indexing; AI model responses became significantly less detailed within two weeks
  • The forum post — the site was defunct but Google still indexed it; she submitted a Google right-to-erasure request and the URL was de-indexed within 3 weeks

30 days after receiving her report, she'd successfully removed 31 of 34 listings. Three data brokers — all US-based — had not responded to removal requests. She escalated these to the ICO.

What changed

Three months on, Sarah's rescan shows a GhostScore of 28 — "Low Profile." The remaining exposure is her LinkedIn profile (intentional), a professional speaking bio she controls, and her current Companies House director listing for an active company.

The things she can't control, she now knows about. The things she could change, she has.

"I think the thing that surprised me most wasn't how much was out there — it was that I could actually do something about it. I assumed this stuff was just permanent."

What Sarah's case illustrates

Sarah isn't unusual. The pattern we see repeatedly is: privately-conscious people who are nonetheless significantly exposed through channels they've never thought to check — old business registrations, long-forgotten forum accounts, electoral roll aggregations, and the invisible infrastructure of data brokers that most people don't know exist.
